Paper 1 Summary:
The research paper aims to understand the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on higher education, specifically on college students. The problem being solved is the lack of quantitative evidence on the effects of the pandemic on students' experiences and expectations. The method used is a survey of approximately 1,500 undergraduate students at Arizona State University, designed to recover the causal impact of the pandemic on students' current and expected outcomes. 

Key results show large negative effects, including 13% of students delaying graduation, 40% losing a job or internship, and 29% expecting to earn less at age 35. The effects are highly heterogeneous, with lower-income students being 55% more likely to delay graduation than their higher-income peers. 

The limitations of the study include the fact that the survey is based on a single institution, although it is one of the largest and most diverse public universities in the US. Additionally, the survey was conducted in late April 2020, and the results may not capture the full impact of the pandemic, which was still evolving at the time. Overall, the study provides evidence of the significant and uneven impact of the pandemic on college students, exacerbating existing socioeconomic divides.

Paper 2 Summary:
The problem being solved in this research paper is the comparison of the effectiveness of online and face-to-face classes in terms of student grades and retention. The method used was a quantitative study involving 885 students in a Midwestern university's technology administration program. The study used a two-factor ANOVA to compare grades and chi-square analysis to compare course retention between online and face-to-face classes. The key results showed no significant difference in grades and course retention between online and face-to-face students. The study also found no significant difference in program retention for students who started the program in online or face-to-face classes. The limitations of the study include the need for further research on different delivery variations and the analysis of specific retention factors affecting students in online education, such as age, gender, and GPA. Overall, the study's findings suggest that online classes can be as effective as face-to-face classes in terms of student grades and retention.

Paper 3 Summary:
The research paper extract discusses a study on the perception of different stakeholders (students, teachers, parents, and head of schools) towards virtual teaching-learning mode during the COVID-19 pandemic at the upper primary stage. 

The problem being solved is the sudden transition from offline to online education due to the pandemic, which posed a significant challenge for stakeholders. The method used involves a qualitative research approach, with the project investigator, Ms. Vandana, conducting a study to determine the perspectives of various stakeholders on online education.

The key results of the study highlight the significance of virtual teaching-learning amid the pandemic and the experiences and perceptions of different stakeholders in the process of online teaching-learning mode. The study provides suggestive measures to make online education more effective by overcoming persisting issues.

However, the limitations of the study are not explicitly stated in the extract. Nevertheless, potential limitations could include the limited scope of the study, which focuses on the upper primary stage, and the potential biases of the stakeholders' perceptions. Overall, the study aims to provide insights into the effectiveness of virtual teaching-learning mode during the pandemic and inform strategies to improve online education.

GAP ANALYSIS:

**1. UNSOLVED PROBLEMS**

These papers admit to several unsolved problems and limitations:

* Paper 1 mentions that the survey is based on a single institution, which might not be representative of all higher education institutions (HEIs). Additionally, the survey was conducted in late April 2020, which may not capture the full impact of the pandemic (Paper 1).
* Paper 2 mentions the need for further research on different delivery variations and the analysis of specific retention factors affecting students in online education, such as age, gender, and GPA (Paper 2).
* Paper 3 does not explicitly state its limitations, but potential limitations could include the limited scope of the study, which focuses on the upper primary stage, and the potential biases of the stakeholders' perceptions (Paper 3).

**2. CONTRADICTIONS**

There are no direct contradictions between the papers, as they focus on different aspects of education during the pandemic. However, Paper 1 highlights the significant and uneven impact of the pandemic on college students, while Paper 2 suggests that online classes can be as effective as face-to-face classes in terms of student grades and retention. This might seem contradictory, but Paper 1 focuses on the overall impact of the pandemic, whereas Paper 2 compares the effectiveness of online and face-to-face classes.

**3. RESEARCH GAPS**

Important questions that none of these papers answer include:

* How do the effects of the pandemic on students' experiences and expectations vary across different institutions, programs, and demographics?
* What are the long-term consequences of the pandemic on students' career prospects and earning potential?
* How can online education be tailored to meet the needs of students with different learning styles, abilities, and socioeconomic backgrounds?
* What are the most effective strategies for supporting students who are struggling with online education, and how can these strategies be implemented at scale?

Experiments that have not been tried include:

* A longitudinal study tracking the impact of the pandemic on students' outcomes over several years
* A comparative study of different online education platforms and their effectiveness in supporting student learning
* An analysis of the impact of the pandemic on students' mental health and well-being, and the effectiveness of different support services in addressing these issues

**4. SUGGESTED DIRECTIONS**

Based on the gaps, here are three specific new research ideas that would be genuinely novel and feasible:

1. **Longitudinal study of pandemic-related trauma on students' mental health and academic outcomes**: This study would track a cohort of students over several years, using a mixed-methods approach to examine the impact of pandemic-related trauma on their mental health, academic outcomes, and career prospects. The study would investigate the effectiveness of different support services, such as counseling and academic support, in mitigating the negative effects of the pandemic.
2. **Comparative analysis of online education platforms for students with disabilities**: This study would compare the effectiveness of different online education platforms in supporting students with disabilities, such as visual or hearing impairments. The study would investigate the accessibility features of each platform, the level of support provided to students with disabilities, and the impact of these platforms on student outcomes, such as grades and retention.
3. **Investigating the impact of pandemic-induced financial stress on students' academic decisions and outcomes**: This study would examine the relationship between pandemic-induced financial stress and students' academic decisions, such as delaying graduation or switching to online programs. The study would use a survey and interview approach to gather data on students' financial situations, academic decisions, and outcomes, and would investigate the effectiveness of different financial support services, such as emergency loans and scholarships, in mitigating the negative effects of financial stress on student outcomes.
